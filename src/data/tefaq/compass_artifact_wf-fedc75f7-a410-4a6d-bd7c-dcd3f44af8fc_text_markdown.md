# TEFAQ: Complete knowledge base for app development

**The TEFAQ (Test d'évaluation de français adapté pour le Québec) is a modular French proficiency exam created by CCI Paris Île-de-France and administered through its Le français des affaires division, uniquely designed for Quebec immigration.** Its defining feature — and the key differentiator from every other French test — is that only the two oral sections (listening and speaking) are mandatory; reading and writing are optional modules that earn additional immigration points. This modularity makes TEFAQ the most strategically efficient path for candidates targeting Quebec exclusively. The test underwent significant format changes in December 2023 (reduced question counts) and September 2025 (simplified listening), meaning older prep materials are partially outdated. Scores range from **0 to 699 per section**, map directly to CEFR levels A1–C2, and the critical immigration threshold is **B2 (score ≥400)** for oral skills under most Quebec programs.

---

## Test architecture: four sections, two mandatory

The TEFAQ comprises four independently scored sections. For Quebec immigration through the Ministère de l'Immigration, de la Francisation et de l'Intégration (MIFI), only listening and speaking are compulsory for the principal applicant. Reading and writing are optional but can significantly boost an immigration application's point total.

| Section | Duration | Questions/Tasks | Format | Score range |
|---------|----------|----------------|--------|-------------|
| Compréhension orale (Listening) | 40 minutes | 40 MCQ | Computer-based | 0–699 |
| Expression orale (Speaking) | 15 minutes | 2 role-play tasks | Face-to-face with examiner | 0–699 |
| Compréhension des écrits (Reading) | 60 minutes | 40 MCQ | Computer-based | 0–699 |
| Expression écrite (Writing) | 60 minutes | 2 writing tasks | Computer-based | 0–699 |

**Total duration is approximately 2 hours 55 minutes** when all four sections are taken. The listening, reading, and writing sections are administered on computers with QWERTY keyboards, integrated virtual accent keyboards, and headphones for audio. The speaking section is conducted face-to-face with a human examiner and systematically audio-recorded for double evaluation. All modules are taken on the same day at an authorized test center — no remote or at-home testing option exists.

Each section is scored independently on the **0–699 scale**. There is no composite or weighted total; each section receives its own CEFR level designation. Correct answers on MCQ sections earn +1 raw point with **no penalty for wrong answers** (no negative marking). Raw scores are then converted to calibrated scores using Item Response Theory (IRT), which accounts for question difficulty differences. For expression sections (speaking and writing), two independent trained evaluators score using standardized grids, and a third evaluator intervenes in cases of significant discrepancy. Results are delivered by email within **2–4 weeks** of the exam date and remain valid for **2 years**. Candidates may retake the exam with a minimum **2-month gap** between sessions.

## Compréhension orale: 40 questions across seven exercise types

The listening section underwent major restructuring in December 2023, dropping from 60 questions to 40. A further simplification in September 2025 reduced answer choices for micro-trottoirs from four to three and allowed two listens for interview segments instead of one. Audio plays automatically and — with the exception of interviews since September 2025 — cannot be replayed.

The 40 questions span **seven exercise types** in progressive difficulty:

| Exercise type | Questions | Content | Difficulty |
|--------------|-----------|---------|------------|
| Conversations with drawings | 4 | Short dialogues paired with images; identify objects, products, everyday situations | Elementary |
| Public announcements | 4 | Train station, airport, cinema, fair announcements; identify purpose/message | Elementary |
| Micro-trottoirs (street interviews) | 6 | Short spontaneous interviews; understand opinions (now 3 answer choices) | Intermediate |
| Radio chronicles | 2 | Short radio commentary pieces | Intermediate |
| Interviews | 6 | Longer interview exchanges (now played twice) | Intermediate–Advanced |
| Reportage (journalistic report) | 1 | A full journalistic report | Advanced |
| Various/diverse documents | ~17 | Mixed formats covering diverse Francophone contexts | Mixed |

**Competencies evaluated** include understanding general and specific information in spoken discourse, grasping global meaning of conversations on both concrete and abstract themes, identifying speaker intent and implied meaning, following speech at native speed, and processing diverse Francophone accents (France, Quebec, Belgium, Africa, Switzerland). The exam now emphasizes **la francophonie dans son ensemble** (Francophonie as a whole), meaning candidates should prepare for accent diversity. Questions test consequences of events, reasons, advice given, target audiences, speaker opinions, and specific factual details like numbers and times.

## Expression orale: two role-plays with a human examiner

The speaking section is the most distinctive component of the TEFAQ. It consists of **two role-play scenarios** — not an interview — conducted face-to-face with a trained examiner. The session is audio-recorded so a second independent examiner can evaluate afterward.

**Section A — Obtain information (5 minutes, formal register).** The candidate receives a short document (an advertisement for an apartment, service, event, or product) and has approximately 1 minute to read it. The candidate then simulates a phone call to the advertiser, with the examiner playing the interlocutor. The candidate must ask **approximately 10 relevant, varied questions** using formal French (*vous* form). Topics to ask about typically include price, schedule, location, duration, requirements, availability, conditions, and what is included. The key skill tested is the ability to conduct a formal information-gathering conversation, seek clarification, and ask follow-up questions.

**Section B — Present, convince, and argue (10 minutes, informal register).** The candidate receives a different document and must present its content to the examiner (playing a skeptical friend), then convince them to participate in the activity described. **The examiner is specifically instructed to disagree and raise objections** — practical, financial, and emotional. The candidate must use informal French (*tu* form), present content clearly, give opinions, argue persuasively with examples, and handle counter-arguments. Using *vous* instead of *tu* in this section is **heavily penalized**.

**Scoring criteria** are divided into two pillars. The first pillar evaluates the quality and effectiveness of discourse: for Section A, this means relevance and completeness of questioning; for Section B, it means development and variety of arguments, determination in defending ideas, and creating a rich, lively conversation. The second pillar evaluates mastery of spoken language across four dimensions: accuracy and appropriateness of vocabulary, mastery of verb tenses and moods (correct subjunctive use is valued), variety of grammatical structures, and pronunciation with natural intonation. Non-verbal communication is explicitly **not assessed**. The final score combines averaged evaluations from both examiners on the 0–699 scale.

## Compréhension des écrits: 40 questions across seven exercises

The reading section was also restructured in December 2023, dropping from 50 to 40 questions. Two exercise types were discontinued (passages to put in order and synonymous sentences). The 60-minute section progresses from elementary to advanced comprehension.

| Exercise | Questions | Content | Difficulty |
|----------|-----------|---------|------------|
| A: Everyday documents | 7 | Ads, menus, notices, brochures, signs — 1 question each | Elementary |
| B: Gap-fill sentences | 6 | Short sentences with a missing word; choose from 4 options | Elementary–Intermediate |
| C: Gap-fill texts | 4 | Two short texts with 2 gaps each; select correct word | Intermediate |
| D: Text-to-information matching | 4 | Match information statements to the correct text among 4 options | Intermediate |
| E: Graph interpretation | 1 | Match a written statement to the correct graph from 4 options | Intermediate |
| F: Administrative/professional documents | 10 | Contracts, business emails, regulations — 2 questions per document | Intermediate–Advanced |
| G: Press articles | 8 | Newspaper/magazine articles — 2 questions per article | Advanced |

Question types test factual/literal comprehension, main idea identification, vocabulary in context, inferential comprehension, author's tone and intent (evaluative comprehension), and referential comprehension (tracking pronouns and connectors). Text themes commonly cover travel, employment, housing, education, environment, technology, health, food, culture, immigration, and consumer services.

## Expression écrite: two writing tasks with a 12-criteria evaluation grid

The writing section lasts 60 minutes and contains two mandatory tasks completed on computer.

**Section A — Le fait divers (25 minutes, minimum 80 words).** The candidate receives the opening lines of a news article (*fait divers*) and must write a coherent continuation. Requirements include producing at least 2 paragraphs, adding genuinely new information (not rephrasing the prompt), maintaining a neutral journalistic tone, and demonstrating appropriate use of past tenses (*passé composé*, *imparfait*). No personal opinions should appear. Typical prompts include scenarios like a discovery while digging in a garden, a zoo escape incident, or an unusual wedding at high altitude.

**Section B — L'argumentation (35 minutes, minimum 200 words).** The candidate receives a provocative statement and must express and justify their point of view. Requirements include at least 3 paragraphs (introduction, body with arguments, conclusion), development of at least 3 distinct arguments, and use of logical connectors. Despite being popularly called "la lettre," the official test creators have clarified that **letter format is not obligatory** — a straightforward argumentative essay is acceptable, and formal letter headers earn zero additional points. Typical prompts include statements like "Il faut absolument supprimer la publicité" or "Les transports publics devraient être gratuits pour tous."

The evaluation grid, confirmed by peer-reviewed academic research (Érudit, *Mesure et évaluation en éducation*, Vol. 39, 2016), comprises **12 criteria** organized into three groups:

- **COMA (3 criteria)** — Communicative criteria for Section A: pertinence of information transmitted, quality of information (development and detail), internal coherence of the narrative
- **COMB (3 criteria)** — Communicative criteria for Section B: pertinence/adherence to prompt, quality of arguments (development, examples, illustrations), internal coherence of argumentation
- **LING (6 criteria)** — Linguistic criteria across both tasks: textual cohesion (connectors, logical progression), sentence-level cohesion (pronoun references, sentence linking), syntactic variety (range of simple and complex structures), vocabulary richness and precision, grammatical accuracy (verb conjugation, agreement, tense usage), spelling and punctuation

The exact point weighting per criterion is proprietary and unpublished, held within CCI Paris Île-de-France's internal "Guide du correcteur." However, both communicative effectiveness and linguistic mastery appear equally important.

## CEFR alignment and Quebec immigration score thresholds

TEFAQ scores map to CEFR levels in consistent **100-point bands** across all four sections:

| TEFAQ score | CEFR level | Échelle québécoise (EQNCF) |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------------|
| 0–99 | Below A1 | — |
| 100–199 | A1 | Niveaux 1–2 |
| 200–259 | A2 | Niveau 3 |
| 260–299 | A2 | Niveau 4 |
| 300–399 | B1 | Niveaux 5–6 |
| 400–499 | B2 | Niveaux 7–8 |
| 500–599 | C1 | Niveaux 9–10 |
| 600–699 | C2 | Niveaux 11–12 |

Quebec uses its own 12-level Échelle québécoise des niveaux de compétence en français (EQNCF), and the correspondence table above is published by the Quebec government (November 2024 version). The TEFAQ is a **positioning test with no pass/fail** — it assigns a score and corresponding level. However, practical "passing" thresholds exist for immigration:

**Programme de sélection des travailleurs qualifiés (PSTQ, effective November 29, 2024):**
- Stream 1 (high qualifications, TEER 0/1/2): **Oral French Level 7+ (B2, score ≥400)** and **Written French Level 5+ (B1, score ≥300)**
- Stream 2 (in-demand occupations, TEER 3/4/5): **Oral French Level 5+ (B1, score ≥300)**
- Spouse/partner: Level 4 oral French (A2, score ≥200)

**Canadian citizenship (via TEFAQ):** Minimum EQNCF Level 4 for both listening and speaking sections only.

The **B2 threshold (score ≥400) in oral skills is the single most important benchmark** for the majority of Quebec immigration pathways and should be the primary target in any TEFAQ preparation app.

## What distinguishes high scorers from average performers

**Listening.** High scorers preview questions before each audio clip to know exactly what information to target. They master Sections A and B (the simpler exercises) completely as a baseline, then strategically pick up points in harder sections. They train specifically with diverse Francophone accents, practice catching numbers, negation, and time expressions on first listen, and never leave questions blank. Average scorers try to understand every word rather than focusing on key information, panic when they miss details, and use generic French listening materials instead of TEF-specific practice.

**Speaking.** High scorers demonstrate clear register awareness — clean *vous* for Section A, natural *tu* for Section B. In Section B, they acknowledge the examiner's objections before countering (*"Je comprends ton point de vue, cependant..."*), offer compromises and creative solutions, vary their vocabulary with synonyms and advanced structures including the subjunctive (*"Bien que tu aies raison..."*), and maintain conversational flow without long pauses. Average scorers use the wrong register, run out of arguments when the examiner pushes back, become aggressive or freeze under pressure, and deliver monologue-style responses instead of natural conversation.

**Reading.** High scorers read questions first, then the text — focusing attention on what matters. They skim for main ideas, scan for specific details, use context clues for unfamiliar vocabulary, and manage time strictly (never spending too long on one question). Average scorers read texts linearly before looking at questions, get stuck on unknown words, and run out of time on the advanced press articles in Section G.

**Writing.** High scorers use clear paragraph structure, employ advanced connectors (*toutefois, néanmoins, en revanche, bien que, malgré*), replace basic vocabulary with precise alternatives (*estimer* instead of *penser*), include concrete examples, maintain formal register throughout, and reserve 5 minutes for proofreading. Average scorers write in one long block, overuse basic connectors (*et, mais*), repeat vocabulary, include anglicisms, go off-topic, and fail to meet word count requirements.

## Preparation resources: official and third-party

**Official resources from Le français des affaires (CCI Paris Île-de-France)** form the authoritative foundation. Free sample papers exist for all four sections as downloadable PDFs (updated October 2024), with accompanying audio files for listening. Four interactive online tutorials simulate the actual exam interface. YouTube workshop replays cover each section in detail. The official Facebook group provides additional sample papers and community support.

The **only officially co-developed online platform** is **PrepMyTEF** by PrepMyFuture, built in partnership with CCI Paris Île-de-France, offering 2,200+ practice questions and official practice tests in real exam conditions (12-month paid subscription). UQAM's Centre d'évaluation de compétences linguistiques explicitly recommends this platform.

The **official training book** is *TEF – Livre d'entraînement* (Hachette FLE, April 2023, ISBN 978-2016286678), directly linked from the Le français des affaires preparation page. The only book specifically titled for TEFAQ is *ABC TEFAQ* by Stéphane Edmond (CLE International, 2019, ISBN 978-2-09-038246-4), containing 216 pages of preparation exercises, a full TEFAQ simulation, and 50 downloadable audio documents. A newer *ABC TEF* (CLE International) covers all TEF variants including TEFAQ in the updated format.

Notable third-party resources include the **Raston's Education TEF Canada Practice Book Series** (multiple volumes covering each section with full-length tests), **TEF Monde: Expression Orale** by Brian Burzynski (a TEF examiner, 2021), and various online platforms including FrenchTestSimulator.com (full exam simulations), tefaq-preparation.ca (sample oral topics and listening breakdowns), and TalkPal AI (interactive practice). UQAM offers a tailored TEFAQ test prep course through its Langues en continu program requiring B1 minimum, and McGill Continuing Studies runs 6-week TEF/TEFAQ prep courses.

For general French skill building, recommended free resources include TV5MONDE's Apprendre le français (2,000+ exercises, A1–C2), RFI's Le français facile (news in simplified French), and Podcast Français Facile (dialogues with transcripts).

## Format changes since 2020 and current test status

The TEFAQ remains actively offered as of April 2026 and has **not been merged with or replaced by any other test**. The Quebec government explicitly lists TEFAQ among accepted tests for the Programme de sélection des travailleurs qualifiés alongside TCF-Québec, TCF-Canada, TCF, DELF, DALF, TEF Canada, and TEF.

The most significant change came on **December 11, 2023**, when CCI Paris Île-de-France restructured the comprehension sections across all TEF variants. Listening dropped from **60 to 40 questions** with new, simpler question types and content emphasizing authentic Francophone media. Reading dropped from **50 to 40 questions**, discontinuing passage-ordering and synonymous-sentence exercises. The expression sections (speaking and writing) were **not changed**. A further **September 2025 update** simplified the listening section: micro-trottoirs now offer 3 answer choices instead of 4, and interview segments allow two listens instead of one.

A notable policy change: since 2022, Quebec (MIFI) now accepts **TEF Canada** results for Quebec immigration applications, giving candidates more flexibility. However, TEFAQ remains strategically advantageous for Quebec-only applicants because of its modular structure (only oral sections mandatory). TEFAQ is also accepted by IRCC for Canadian citizenship but is **not accepted** for federal immigration programs like Express Entry.

## How TEFAQ compares with competing French tests

**TEFAQ vs. TEF Canada.** These are sibling variants from the same creator (CCI Paris), sharing identical scoring scales (0–699), CEFR alignment, and question formats. The critical difference is modularity: TEFAQ requires only 2 mandatory sections (listening + speaking) while TEF Canada requires all 4. TEF Canada is more versatile (accepted for both federal and Quebec immigration), but TEFAQ is more efficient for Quebec-only applicants. Cost reflects this: TEFAQ oral-only runs **$200–$300 CAD** versus **$350–$450 CAD** for TEF Canada's full battery.

**TEFAQ vs. TCF Canada.** TCF Canada is administered by France Éducation international (French Ministry of Education), not CCI Paris. Key strategic differences: TCF listening plays audio first and then shows questions, while TEFAQ allows previewing questions before audio plays — a significant advantage for strategic listeners. TCF speaking uses three tasks of progressive difficulty (personal interview → interactive task → viewpoint expression), while TEFAQ uses two role-play scenarios (phone call + persuasion). TCF scores speaking and writing on a 0–20 scale rather than 0–699. Both have Quebec-specific variants accepted by MIFI.

**TEFAQ vs. DELF/DALF.** The fundamental difference is that DELF/DALF are **diploma exams** (you select a level and pass or fail) with **lifetime validity**, while TEFAQ is a **positioning test** (you receive whatever score you earn) valid for only 2 years. DELF/DALF require all 4 skills mandatorily. TEFAQ's score-based approach is lower-risk since any result earns some immigration points, while failing a DELF/DALF yields no certificate at all. Both DELF and DALF are accepted by MIFI for Quebec immigration.

## App development implications and product recommendations

For a web app specifically targeting TEFAQ candidates, the knowledge base above suggests several key product features. A **simulated test mode** should mirror the exact current format: 40 MCQ listening questions with single-play audio across seven exercise types, 40 MCQ reading questions across seven exercises with progressive difficulty, timed writing tasks with word counters (80-word and 200-word minimums), and role-play speaking simulations with AI acting as a pushback-giving examiner. The **B2 score threshold (≥400)** should be the default target, with clear progress tracking against this benchmark.

The app should incorporate register-switching practice (formal *vous* ↔ informal *tu*) as this is a unique TEFAQ requirement, argumentation frameworks for both speaking and writing with template structures and advanced connector libraries, diverse Francophone accent training for the listening section, and the specific seven exercise types for both listening and reading to match the actual exam structure. A diagnostic assessment should map users to their current CEFR level and estimate preparation time to B2 based on the empirical finding that A1→B2 takes approximately 12–14 months with consistent daily practice.

The scoring engine should replicate CEFR-level banding (100-point increments on the 0–699 scale) and display results in both CEFR levels and Échelle québécoise equivalents, since Quebec immigration uses its own 12-level scale. Practice writing evaluation should assess the 12-criteria grid (3 communicative criteria per task + 6 linguistic criteria across both), and speaking evaluation should score on both discourse quality/effectiveness and language mastery pillars.

## Conclusion

The TEFAQ occupies a unique niche among French proficiency tests: it is the only major exam where candidates can qualify for immigration by demonstrating mastery of just oral skills. This modularity, combined with its score-based (not pass/fail) structure, makes it the lowest-risk, most focused path for Quebec immigration candidates. The December 2023 and September 2025 format updates have made the exam slightly more accessible (fewer questions, simplified listening), but the core challenge remains achieving B2 in listening and speaking — sections where even candidates comfortable in daily French conversation often underperform. Any preparation tool should center on the role-play speaking format (the most anxiety-inducing and format-dependent section), diverse-accent listening practice, and the specific B2 threshold of 400/699 that gates most immigration pathways.
