## THE EVALUATION STRUCTURE: TWO PILLARS, FIVE CRITERIA

The official CCI Paris grading grid assesses performance on **two separate axes**, which break down into **five distinct scored criteria** (confirmed by ATF Montréal, active examiners).

### Pillar 1 — Quality and Efficacy of Discourse (2 criteria, one per section)

These criteria assess whether the candidate is doing the *right communicative things* for each section.

**Criterion 1A — Section A: Discourse quality / Information-gathering ability**
What examiners evaluate:
- Did the candidate ask questions that were relevant to the document given?
- Did they cover all possible aspects of the subject (pertinence AND completeness)?
- Did they react appropriately to the examiner's answers?
- Did they ask for clarification or elaboration when answers were vague ("Qu'entendez-vous par...? Pourriez-vous préciser...?")?
- Did they maintain a **formal register** (consistent *vouvoiement*, polite framing) throughout?
- Were reactions spontaneous and natural, or did they feel scripted?

**Criterion 1B — Section B: Discourse quality / Argumentation efficacy**
What examiners evaluate:
- Did the candidate develop a proper **introduction** that presented the document and framed the activity?
- Were arguments **well-developed and varied** (not short and repetitive)?
- Did they demonstrate **détermination dans la défense des idées** — conviction and engagement, not capitulation?
- Did they respond to counter-arguments with **acknowledgment + pivot**, not avoidance or repetition?
- Was the conversation **rich and animated** — natural exchange between friends, not a monologue?
- Did they offer **creative solutions and compromises** when the examiner resisted?
- Did they maintain **informal register** (*tutoiement*, casual but polite, *registre familier/courant*)?

### Pillar 2 — Richness and Mastery of the French Language (3 criteria, applied across both sections)

**Criterion 2A — Le lexique (Vocabulary)**
- Is vocabulary **precise and appropriate** to the context?
- Does the candidate use **varied vocabulary** or repeat the same words?
- Does their vocabulary reflect their target level? (B1: adequate, B2: rich and idiomatic, C1: nuanced and sophisticated)
- Are words correctly matched to the situation (register-appropriate)?

**Criterion 2B — La syntaxe (Grammar and sentence structure)**
- Are sentences varied or all identical/simple?
- Are **verb tenses and moods used correctly**?
- Is the **subjunctive used appropriately** when called for? (Correct subjunctive use is positively weighted)
- Do they use complex structures (*bien que + subjonctif*, conditional sentences, relative clauses)?
- Are basic errors (agreement, conjugation, auxiliary selection) frequent or rare?

**Criterion 2C — L'aisance à l'oral (Fluency and Pronunciation)**
- Is speech **smooth without excessive hesitation or long silences**?
- Is pronunciation **clear and intelligible**? (Note: accent is NOT penalized as long as it is intelligible)
- Does intonation match the communicative situation? (Emphasis on key words when defending an argument signals conviction)
- Does the candidate speak at an appropriate pace?

### Scoring mechanic
Each of the 5 criteria is individually assigned a CEFR sub-level from A1- to C2+. These sub-level scores are converted to points and **averaged** to produce the final 0–699 calibrated score. This means weak performance on one criterion can be compensated by strong performance on others — the score is **holistic, not pass/fail per criterion**.

## THE SEVEN EVALUATIVE QUESTIONS EXAMINERS ASK THEMSELVES
(Directly from the official CCI Paris PDF, October 2024 — these are printed on the evaluation overview page)

1. Is the discourse **adapted to the situation**?
2. Are the ideas **summarily expressed, vague, precise, or detailed**?
3. Are reactions to examiner interventions **adapted, spontaneous, natural**?
4. Is the discourse **clear, lucid, confused, or incoherent**?
5. Is the vocabulary used **accurate, precise, adequate**?
6. Are sentences **simple, complex, mastered**?
7. Are tenses and moods **correctly used**?
8. Is pronunciation **clear, natural**?

These eight questions (the PDF lists them around the grid overview graphic) are the mental checklist every examiner applies in real time. They map directly to the 5 criteria.

## SECTION A: DEEP DIVE

### Format
- Duration: 5 minutes
- Document type: Everyday advertisement — job offer, service, leisure activity, housing, spectacle, etc.
- Instruction template: *"Vous avez lu une annonce pour XXXXXXXX et vous êtes intéressé(e). Vous téléphonez pour avoir plus d'informations. → Posez une dizaine de questions."*
- Register: FORMAL. *Vouvoiement* throughout. Polite framing.
- The examiner plays the role of the person being called (employer, agency, organizer).

### What "une dizaine de questions" means
"Une dizaine" means approximately 10 — it is a target, not a hard minimum. However, asking fewer than 7-8 meaningful questions will clearly hurt the Criterion 1A score. The instruction signals expected depth. Candidates should be aiming for 10+ questions by treating each answer as an opportunity for a follow-up.

### What makes a good Section A performance
- **Varied question types**: not just yes/no questions, but open questions (*Comment... ? Pourquoi... ? Qu'est-ce que... ? Dans quelle mesure... ?*), conditional questions (*Serait-il possible de... ?*), follow-up questions that build on prior answers
- **Covering all natural information categories**: price, schedule/timing, location, duration, requirements/conditions, what is included, availability, contact procedures, cancellation policy, practical logistics
- **Reformulation and clarification**: when the examiner gives a vague or short answer, the candidate asks for more detail ("*Pourriez-vous me préciser... ? Qu'entendez-vous exactement par... ? Et concrètement, ça veut dire quoi ?*")
- **Natural opening**: greeting, stating purpose, positioning oneself as genuinely interested (establishes credibility of the scenario)
- **Follow-through**: questions that react to what was just said, not a prepared list read mechanically

### What kills Section A performance
- Reading from a mental script with no connection to what the examiner said
- Using only generic questions that work for any topic (immediately signals memorization to examiners)
- Starting with "Où se trouve votre établissement?" — too cliché, too generic, signals pre-planned script
- Using *tutoiement* (incorrect register for a professional phone call)
- Running out of questions before the 5 minutes are up with no strategy to generate more
- Asking the same question twice in different words
- Not reacting to prompts: if the examiner volunteers unexpected information, a good candidate builds on it

### Common Section A document types and typical question categories
- **Job offer**: responsibilities, hours, salary, contract type, required qualifications, start date, team size, training provided, career prospects, application process
- **Housing/apartment**: price, charges included, surface area, floor, furnishings, kitchen equipment, heating type, parking, pets allowed, deposit, lease length, neighbourhood
- **Leisure/activity**: price, schedule, duration, age/level requirements, what to bring, group size, guide provided, location, registration process, cancellation policy
- **Spectacle/event**: ticket price, date/time options, venue, duration, age restrictions, accessibility, transport, parking
- **Volunteer/association**: time commitment, activities, training, requirements, who it's for, contact

## SECTION B: DEEP DIVE

### Format
- Duration: 10 minutes
- Document type: Everyday advertisement — similar types to Section A
- Instruction template: *"Vous avez lu une annonce pour XXXXXXXXX. Vous en parlez à un(e) ami(e) qui XXXXXXXXX. → Présentez-lui ce document ; → Essayez de le (la) convaincre de XXXXXXXXX."*
- Register: INFORMAL. *Tutoiement* throughout. Friendly, natural, casual but not crude.
- The examiner plays the role of a **skeptical friend who is instructed to resist**.

### The examiner's role in Section B — critical for AI replication
The examiner is not a passive participant. They are **actively instructed to disagree and raise objections**. Their resistance is systematic and follows three predictable categories:

1. **Practical objection**: *"Je n'ai pas le temps." / "C'est trop loin." / "Je ne peux pas le week-end." / "Je n'aime pas ça."*
2. **Financial objection**: *"C'est trop cher." / "Je n'ai pas le budget." / "Je préfère économiser."*
3. **Emotional/motivational objection**: *"Ça ne m'intéresse pas vraiment." / "Je ne me vois pas faire ça." / "J'ai peur." / "Je ne suis pas prêt(e)."*

The examiner will cycle through these objections as the candidate responds. They do not say "yes" before the time is up. The goal is never to get the friend to say yes — it is to demonstrate **sustained persuasion ability** over 10 minutes.

### The four structural stages of Section B (from french-b2.com, TEF examiner instructor)
1. **Introduction / La présentation** — Present the document naturally and enthusiastically. Give the friend the core information. Set up the offer attractively.
2. **Argumentation principale** — Develop 2-3 strong, varied arguments for WHY the friend should do this. Each argument should have substance (not just "c'est bien").
3. **Gestion des objections** — Listen to the examiner's pushback, acknowledge it, counter it, offer alternatives or compromises.
4. **Conclusion / Wrap-up** — As time runs out, close naturally even without a "yes". *"Je te laisse y réfléchir. On en reparle demain ?"*

### What makes a good Section B performance
- **Enthusiastic, natural opening**: *"Salut ! Écoute, j'ai vu quelque chose d'incroyable et j'ai tout de suite pensé à toi !"*
- **Clear document presentation** before arguing — many candidates jump straight to arguments without grounding the friend in what the activity actually is
- **Arguments that are SPECIFIC to the document**, not generic phrases that could apply to anything
- **Varied argument types**: practical benefit, emotional benefit, social/relational benefit (*"On pourrait y aller ensemble"*), timing benefit (*"C'est exactement ce dont tu as besoin en ce moment"*)
- **Concession + counter pattern**: *"Je comprends tout à fait, mais... / C'est vrai que c'est un inconvénient, cependant... / Tu as raison sur ce point, pourtant..."*
- **Offering creative compromises**: *"Et si on essayait juste une fois pour voir ?" / "On pourrait partager les frais" / "Tu n'as pas besoin de t'engager pour longtemps"*
- **Maintaining the relationship warmth**: friendly encouragement, humour if natural, *"Fais-moi confiance !"*, *"Je t'assure que ça vaut la peine"*
- **Using the subjunctive correctly when conceding**: *"Bien que tu aies raison sur ce point..." / "Même si tu ne te sens pas prêt..."* — this is actively rewarded by examiners

### What kills Section B performance
**The single most penalized error**: Using *"vous"* in Section B. Per PrepMyFrench (examiner-written): it **severely penalizes the score**. It is not a minor slip — it demonstrates a fundamental failure to manage register, which is a core evaluation criterion.

Other major errors:
- **Monologue**: delivering a prepared speech without listening to the examiner's objections (directly observed and heavily penalized — the interaction score drops to near-zero)
- **Capitulating**: when the examiner objects, saying "D'accord, c'est vrai" and not counter-arguing — scored as zero determination
- **Aggression or frustration**: becoming insistent or irritated when the friend disagrees — penalizes both discourse quality and vocabulary/tone
- **Repetition of arguments**: cycling through the same two arguments repeatedly without variation
- **Generic arguments**: arguing things that work for any topic without engaging the specific document ("C'est une bonne activité. Ça va te faire du bien." — this scores at B1 at best)
- **Ignoring the objection**: responding to "C'est trop cher" with an argument about health benefits without addressing the financial concern
- **Forgetting the presentation stage**: diving straight into arguments without first presenting the document
- **Running out of content at minute 4**: the full 10 minutes must be filled with dynamic exchange

## REGISTER: THE MOST DECISIVE SINGLE VARIABLE

Register is assessed under Criterion 1A and 1B (discourse quality / situational adaptation) AND under Criterion 2A (vocabulary adequacy). A register error therefore impacts **two separate criteria simultaneously**.

### What register means in TEFAQ context
- **Section A (formal)**: *vouvoiement*, polite distancing markers, formal question forms (*Serait-il possible de... ? Pourriez-vous... ? Auriez-vous... ?*), absence of casual filler words
- **Section B (informal)**: *tutoiement*, casual connectors (*bon, ben, écoute, dis donc, tu sais*), friend-appropriate register (*Fais-moi confiance ! C'est trop bien !*)

### Severity of register errors
- **Systematic vous in Section B**: severe penalty across Criterion 1B and 2A. Per PrepMyFrench (examiner): "severely penalize your score"
- **One slip of vous in Section B**: lower penalty but still noted — consistency is expected
- **Overly formal vocabulary in Section B**: penalized under Criterion 2A for inadequacy (vocabulary not matched to context)
- **Informal/casual vocabulary in Section A**: penalized similarly — using *"super"* or *"t'inquiète"* in a professional phone call context is a register error

## CEFR LEVEL BAND DESCRIPTORS FOR SPEAKING

### A1/A2 (scores 100–299) — Observable behaviors
- Very short, fragmented responses
- Limited to known fixed phrases
- Long silences; needs significant time to formulate
- Frequent comprehension failures requiring the examiner to repeat
- Section A: asks fewer than 5 questions; all very basic
- Section B: cannot sustain argument for 10 minutes; stops at 3-4 minutes

### B1 (scores 300–399) — Observable behaviors
- Can communicate on familiar topics but with visible effort
- Uses basic connectors correctly (et, mais, parce que, alors, donc)
- Vocabulary adequate but limited; same words repeated
- Section A: asks 6-8 questions, mostly basic; some missed follow-ups
- Section B: can argue for the activity but becomes stuck when objections hit; repeats same arguments; low variety
- Grammar: present, past (passé composé), future; avoids complex structures
- Subjunctive: absent or incorrect
- Discourse is generally comprehensible but not fluid

### B2 (scores 400–499) — THE IMMIGRATION TARGET
**This is the critical band. Examiners confirm it is the hardest to consistently assess.**

Observable behaviors that CONFIRM B2:
- Connects ideas fluently with varied discourse markers (*d'abord, par ailleurs, en revanche, cependant, néanmoins, pourtant*)
- Handles objections with the **concession + counter** pattern without hesitation
- Varied vocabulary — uses synonyms, avoids repetition
- **Subjunctive used correctly in natural speech** (*bien que, à condition que, pour que, il faut que*)
- Section A: 9-12 varied questions; covers all major information categories; asks follow-ups when answers are vague
- Section B: sustains the full 10 minutes with energy; offers at least 4-5 varied arguments; adapts to each specific objection; offers compromises
- Self-corrects smoothly when making errors (does not disrupt flow)
- Register is consistently appropriate throughout
- Pronunciation clear; natural intonation; accent may be present but never impedes comprehension
- Spontaneity visible — reacts to what the examiner says rather than sticking to a plan

From learnfrenchinvancouver.com: *"The difference between B1 and B2 is often structural. B2 speakers connect their ideas."* A B1 speaker says: *"C'est bien. Ça coûte pas cher. C'est pas loin."* A B2 speaker says: *"Non seulement c'est accessible financièrement, mais en plus la localisation est parfaite pour toi. D'ailleurs, je me souviens que tu m'avais dit que tu cherchais quelque chose comme ça."*

### C1 (scores 500–599) — Observable behaviors
- Natural, near-fluent delivery with minimal self-correction
- Rich, precise vocabulary including idiomatic expressions
- Complex sentence structures used automatically
- In Section B: uses persuasion strategies beyond argument (storytelling, emotional appeal, humor, shared experience references)
- Registers switch seamlessly within the same section when appropriate
- Can handle unexpected turns in the conversation without losing composure
- Discourse is organized and coherent even when improvising

## DOUBLE EVALUATION AND SCORING PROCESS

### How two examiners score the same performance
- Examiner 1 is present during the test and conducts the role-play. They evaluate WHILE the test is happening.
- Examiner 2 listens to the audio recording of the test afterward. They evaluate without knowing Examiner 1's score.
- Both use the same standardized grid.
- The final score is a **processed average** of both evaluations (exact formula is proprietary).
- If there is a **significant discrepancy** between the two scores, a **third examiner** intervenes as arbitrator.

### Academic findings on inter-rater variability (Ince, 2022 — UdeM doctoral thesis)
This is the most rigorous independent academic study of TEF oral evaluation. Key findings:
- **Discrepancies are present and documented**: two examiners can give the same score while their interpretations differ, and vice versa
- **Accent bias**: some examiners are positively influenced when the candidate's accent is familiar to them
- **Irrelevant inferences**: examiners sometimes construct explanations for a candidate's difficulty that are not grounded in the actual performance
- **Examiner attitude effect**: how the examiner behaves DURING the conversation (their energy, responsiveness) can affect the candidate's performance and thus their score
- **The B1/B2 boundary is the most contested**: examiners reported the most disagreement at this level — partly because of its immigration stakes (B2 gives immigration points, B1 does not)
- **One criterion specifically criticized**: the doctoral research identified that one criterion contained "two distinct actions" (likely the vocabulary criterion covering both *précision* and *adéquation*), leading to inconsistent interpretation
- **Descriptors judged ambiguous or vague** by the examiners themselves — some descriptors were interpreted differently across the 10 examiners studied

**App implication**: Because the B1/B2 boundary is the most contested and highest-stakes zone, your AI evaluator should be especially precise and evidence-based in feedback given to candidates in the 350–450 range. Show them exactly which specific behaviors push them into B2 vs. hold them at B1.

## THE TOP 10 EXAMINER-DOCUMENTED ERRORS (Franco Feels — 7-year active TEFAQ examiner, Anaïs Laurent)

1. **Memorizing fixed phrases and repeating them** — Examiners develop a "memorization radar." Scripted speech immediately signals a false level. Examiners hear the same memorized phrases (with the same errors) repeatedly. "Before being an examiner, the person evaluating you is a teacher — we know immediately when someone is reciting."

2. **Not interacting with the examiner — doing a monologue** — The fundamental nature of the TEFAQ oral test is INTERACTION. Candidates who ignore the examiner's answers and deliver prepared content score far below their actual French level.

3. **Not knowing the exercise format before arriving** — Candidates with solid French discover exercises live and cannot perform them correctly. This is wasted potential.

4. **Asking generic questions or using arguments that work for any topic** — Starting Section A with "Où se trouve votre établissement?" is the classic giveaway. It tells the examiner the candidate has a list, not a conversation. B2 requires specific, contextualized questions and arguments.

5. **Going to the exam unprepared "just to try"** — A1/B1-level candidates who sit the test hoping to pass waste money, lose confidence, and create psychological barriers to the next attempt.

6. **Not preparing with an expert examiner** — The test has specific rules and expectations that only experienced evaluators know internally. Standard French tutoring does not prepare for these.

7. **Believing TEFAQ myths** — Common false beliefs: "Center X is easier than Center Y." "Using 2 conditionals and 3 subjunctives guarantees B2." "Topics are related to current events." These misalign preparation.

8. **Lacking imagination / not improvising** — "What you say at the TEFAQ doesn't need to reflect your real opinion, situation, or interests." Candidates who only say true things about themselves run out of content. **The examiner will never contradict you — they evaluate your ability to express yourself, regardless of whether what you say is true.** Invent examples, details, even stories if needed.

9. **Looking for a "fast track"** — The failure spiral: no prep → fail → quick fix → fail again. Sustainable progress requires months of consistent practice, not exam cramming.

10. **Forgetting the goal is to learn French** — Exam technique can only amplify an existing level. Candidates who work only on exam strategy without building real French proficiency eventually hit a ceiling.

## WHAT THE AI DIALOGUE EVALUATOR NEEDS TO DETECT

Based on the rubric above, here is the programmatic spec for an AI evaluator scoring a TEFAQ-style speaking simulation.

### Section A AI evaluation inputs
| What to detect | Maps to | Scoring signal |
|---|---|---|
| Number of questions asked | Criterion 1A completeness | <7 → below B1; 7-9 → B1; 10+ → B2 possible |
| Question variety (open vs closed, follow-up vs new topic) | Criterion 1A pertinence | Only closed yes/no → B1 ceiling; open + follow-up → B2 indicator |
| Use of *vouvoiement* throughout | Criterion 1A + 2A | ANY *tutoiement* in Section A = register penalty |
| Categories covered (price, schedule, conditions, location, etc.) | Criterion 1A completeness | Missing major category = completeness deduction |
| Follow-up questions after examiner answers | Criterion 1A natural interaction | Absent → monologue penalty |
| Clarification requests (*Pourriez-vous préciser... ?*) | Criterion 1A | Present → B2 indicator |
| Vocabulary register (formal markers vs casual) | Criterion 2A | Informal vocabulary = register mismatch penalty |
| Subjunctive use | Criterion 2B | Correct use = B2+ indicator |

### Section B AI evaluation inputs
| What to detect | Maps to | Scoring signal |
|---|---|---|
| Use of *tutoiement* | Criterion 1B + 2A | ANY *vouvoiement* = severe register penalty |
| Opening / document presentation present? | Criterion 1B discourse structure | Absent = structure penalty |
| Number of distinct arguments (not repetitions) | Criterion 1B argument variety | <3 → B1; 3-4 → B1/B2 boundary; 5+ varied → B2 |
| Concession + counter pattern | Criterion 1B determination | Absent = B1; present = B2 indicator |
| Argues specific to the document vs generic | Criterion 1B | Generic = memorization flag; specific = B2 indicator |
| Offers compromise or solution | Criterion 1B | Strong B2 indicator |
| Response reacts to specific objection raised | Criterion 1B interaction | Ignoring objection content = non-interactive penalty |
| Variety of persuasion vocabulary | Criterion 2A | Same vocabulary = B1; varied register-appropriate = B2 |
| Discourse connectors (adversative, concessive, causal) | Criterion 2B | Absent/basic → B1; varied → B2 |
| Subjunctive with concessive structures | Criterion 2B | Correct = strong B2/C1 indicator |
| Sentence structure complexity | Criterion 2B | All simple = B1; complex structures = B2 |
| Long pauses / repetition fillers | Criterion 2C | Frequent = B1; rare = B2+ |
| Self-correction | Criterion 2C | Smooth self-correction = B2 indicator |

### Feedback message framework
Feedback should be structured around the 5 criteria, not generic "good job / needs improvement." For each criterion, tell the user:
- Current level signal (A2 / B1 / B2 / C1)
- Specific evidence from their response (quote what they said)
- What B2 would look like (concrete example of the upgraded version)

Example feedback for Section B Criterion 1B:
> "Tes arguments étaient pertinents mais tu as répété les mêmes deux idées (prix et accessibilité) trois fois. À B2, on attend au moins 4-5 arguments distincts. Quand l'examinateur a dit 'Je n'ai pas le temps', tu as continué à parler du prix — à B2, il faut répondre directement à l'objection soulevée. Essaie : 'Je comprends que tu sois chargé(e), mais justement, cette activité ne prend que 2 heures le samedi matin — ça ne perturbera pas ton emploi du temps.'"

## KNOWN EXAMINER BIASES — IMPORTANT FOR AI CALIBRATION

1. **Accent familiarity bias**: Examiners are more lenient with accents they recognize. AI should be accent-neutral.
2. **B1/B2 boundary ambiguity**: Human examiners show the most disagreement here. AI should be MORE precise at this boundary, not less.
3. **Memorization detection**: Examiners are specifically trained to detect scripted speech. AI should flag repetition of multi-word fixed phrases and note it explicitly in feedback.
4. **Examiner attitude effect**: The quality of the AI's "examiner" performance (how realistic, how persistent, how varied the objections) directly affects the candidate's ability to demonstrate their level. A passive AI examiner in Section B artificially inflates candidate performance.

## IMPLEMENTATION NOTES FOR THE LIVE DIALOGUE FEATURE

### The AI examiner for Section B must:
- Begin with genuine enthusiasm about being called / greeted
- After the opening, ask a basic question to test if the candidate presents the document clearly
- After hearing the pitch, raise the first objection (practical: "Je n'ai pas le temps")
- After a counter-argument, acknowledge it briefly but raise a second objection (financial or emotional)
- After each response, cycle to a new angle — NEVER say yes before 8 minutes have elapsed
- If the candidate stops or goes silent for more than 15 seconds, prompt them: "Et alors ?" or "Tu me convaincs pas encore..."
- Wrap up naturally around 10 minutes regardless of outcome

### The AI examiner for Section A must:
- Play a real person who was contacted (answer the phone naturally)
- Give **minimal information** in initial answers to force follow-up questions
- When asked a question, give a brief but real answer — never volunteer information the candidate didn't ask for
- If the candidate makes a register error (uses *tu*), continue naturally (do not flag it — that is the evaluator's role, not the interlocutor's)
- Occasionally give a vague or incomplete answer to test whether the candidate asks for clarification

### Scoring transparency principle
The B2 target (score ≥400) should always be visible as the reference point. After each practice session, show:
- Performance per criterion (color-coded A2 / B1 / B2 / C1)
- The specific behaviors that moved them up or held them back
- One prioritized action item for next practice session
